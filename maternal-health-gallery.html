Maternal Fat Ultrasound & Gestational Outcomes — Power BI Dashboards

A Power BI dashboard suite analyzing 272 pregnancy cases from the PhysioNet maternal ultrasound and nutrition cohort, examining how maternal fat, demographics, lifestyle, lab values, and pregnancy history relate to gestational complications and newborn outcomes.

Predictive analytics identifying the key drivers of newborn weight was performed in Python, with the resulting feature-importance insights visualized directly within the dashboards below.

Demographics Analysis

  • 272 patients tracked, 211 with follow-up (61 lost to follow-up) — average age 26.08, obesity rate 26.47%
  • BMI status shifted in 19.23% of patients from Normal to Overweight and 8.14% from Normal to Obese between pre-pregnancy and current measurement
  • Ethnicity skews White (51.84%) and Black (29.04%); the 18–25 age group shows the highest prevalence of overweight/obese BMI, correlating with elevated pre-eclampsia risk
  • Younger mothers (18–25) trend toward normal birth weight, while higher maternal BMI shifts outcomes toward low birth weight or macrosomia

Dietary Habits and Hospital Outcomes Analysis

  • 192 patients eat 3 meals daily, 59.9% eat fruit/veg daily; 25 confirmed gestational diabetes (GDM) cases; average hospital stay of 1.09 days
  • GDM rate is highest among vaginal deliveries with episiotomy (52.13%) and lowest among standard vaginal deliveries
  • Fetal macrosomia and elective C-section are the top drivers of GDM rate, ahead of cephalopelvic disproportion and unsuccessful induction
  • A healthy-habits pathway (diet/meals) traces clearly into delivery mode and diabetes outcomes

Medical History and Lifestyle Dashboard

  • Hypertension rate 4.04%, tobacco use 19.49%, alcohol use 16.91%, 9 confirmed diabetes patients
  • Asian participants report the highest smoking rate; Black participants report the highest alcohol use
  • A weak positive correlation exists between alcohol consumption and BMI
  • ~73% of hypertensive patients never received treatment, vs. 27% treated — and most diabetes cases lack a documented diabetes gap, with diet/medicine split roughly evenly among treated cases

Anthropometry and Pregnancy History Analysis

  • Average weight gain: 2.09kg (1st→2nd trimester), 6.46kg (2nd→3rd), 9.18kg (1st→3rd) — with a strong positive correlation between 1st and 3rd trimester weight
  • 18.05% of patients have a prior miscarriage on record
  • Half of patients fall into the "Low Gain" weight category by 3rd trimester, ahead of "Healthy" (24%) and "High Gain" (12%)
  • Preterm births skew toward the 18–25 age group, while full-term births are more evenly distributed across age groups

Lab Test and Vitals Analysis

  • Syphilis 5.15%, anaemia risk 13.97%, hepatitis 1.10%
  • GDM mothers show consistently higher glucose across all tests, with the widest gap at OGTT 1h
  • Fasting glucose exceeds diagnostic thresholds in ~20% of cases by the 2nd/3rd trimester, more than either OGTT measure
  • Fasting glucose is highest among 1st-trimester hypertensive patients and generally decreases by the 3rd trimester in normal-BP patients

Predictive Drivers of Newborn Weight

  • Systolic blood pressure is the single strongest predictor of newborn weight (~21% impact), ahead of preperitoneal visceral fat (~19%) and subcutaneous fat (~14%)
  • Central adiposity measures (visceral + subcutaneous fat) combine for roughly one-third of total predictive impact
  • 2nd-trimester fasting glucose (~12%) outweighs 1st (~5%) and 3rd trimester (~2%) readings, suggesting mid-pregnancy glucose control matters most
  • Early hematocrit and total fat measures contribute modestly; later-trimester measures are comparatively weak signals

Newborn and Ultrasound Dashboard

  • Average newborn weight 3.26kg (range 1.11–4.53kg); 169 newborns rated "Normal" health vs. 103 "At Risk"
  • 26.84% of births fall below the low-birth-weight threshold; 12.87% involved newborn aspiration, 1.47% required intubation
  • Term births dominate the normal-weight category, while underweight and overweight outcomes skew toward preterm births
  • The fetal weight percentile distribution is concentrated around the 50th percentile, with smaller shares in extreme percentile bands

Summary of Insights

Across the 272-case cohort, maternal blood pressure and central adiposity (visceral and subcutaneous fat) emerged as the strongest predictors of newborn weight — together accounting for over half of the predictive signal identified through Python-based feature-importance analysis. Gestational diabetes risk tracked closely with delivery complications such as fetal macrosomia and elective C-section, while glucose control in the second trimester proved more consequential than first- or third-trimester readings.

Demographic and lifestyle patterns also shaped outcomes: younger mothers (18–25) showed both higher rates of preterm birth and a higher prevalence of overweight/obese BMI, and a meaningful share of hypertensive and diabetic patients went untreated during pregnancy — pointing to a gap between diagnosis and intervention. Taken together, the dashboards suggest that early monitoring of blood pressure, adiposity, and second-trimester glucose, paired with more consistent treatment follow-through, could meaningfully improve gestational and neonatal outcomes in this population.
